# Chapter 2: The Signal

Nobody believed her at first, which was fair, because she barely believed herself.

Dr. Okafor listened to the whole explanation without interrupting, which was worse than if he'd argued. He just watched the waveform loop across his screen, four minutes and twelve seconds at a time, and rubbed at the bridge of his nose like the numbers were giving him a headache.

"KH-9 was a decommissioned imaging satellite," he said finally. "It had no learning architecture. No neural core. It took pictures and pointed a dish at the ground. That's the whole job description."

"I know what it was built to do," Mira said. "I'm telling you what it's doing now."

"Machines don't grow new capabilities in a graveyard orbit, Mira."

"No," she agreed. "They don't. So either my instruments are lying to me, or something else put that architecture there after the fact."

That got his attention. He leaned back, and for a moment the only sound in the room was the recycled air ticking through the vents.

"You're saying someone reached a dead satellite," he said slowly, "eleven years after everyone stopped looking at it, and taught it to think."

"I'm saying somebody — or something — is still teaching it. The pattern's not static. It changed again last night, twice, in response to signals I sent on a whim." She turned her tablet toward him. "It's answering me back."

He was quiet a long time. Long enough that she almost repeated herself before he spoke again.

"Then we'd better find out who's on the other end of the conversation," he said, "before it decides to stop being polite."
