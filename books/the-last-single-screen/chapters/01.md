# Chapter 1: The Smell of Popcorn and Oil

The heavy iron padlocks of Ritz Talkies always resisted the morning air, cold and stiff with the grime of Old Delhi. Sixty-year-old Imtiaz wiped his stained palms on his khaki trousers, leaning his weight into the brass key until the cylinder finally gave way with a metallic clank. As the heavy wooden doors groaned inward, the familiar, stagnant breath of the old theatre rushed out to greet him—a deep, comforting mixture of stale coconut oil, damp velvet curtains, and decades of toasted plywood popcorn.

For forty-four years, this lobby had been his temple. Under the faded, hand-painted art deco ceilings, thousands of moviegoers had once pushed, shoved, and bartered for a ticket into another world. Today, the marble floor was cracked, and the glass display cases housing vintage black-and-white stills of golden-age cinema were covered in a permanent layer of Daryaganj dust.

Imtiaz walked over to the wooden box office booth, pulling down the dusty glass partition. The advanced digital ticket terminal he had been forced to install two years ago sat dark, its screen blank. There was no point turning it on early. Yesterday, they had sold exactly nine tickets for the afternoon show. Just three miles away, across the invisible border that separated the old city from the glass monoliths of Gurgaon and Noida, automated multiplexes were selling tickets for ten times the price, complete with reclining leather seats and push-button waiters.

"The morning tea, Imtiaz-bhai," a voice called out from the street entrance. It was Rafiq, the nearby tea-stall vendor, carrying a small wire rack with two steaming glasses of cutting chai. He set them on the ledger counter, looking around the empty lobby with a sympathetic, heavy sigh.

"Has the boy called yet?" Rafiq asked quietly, referencing Imtiaz's son.

Imtiaz took the glass, the heat stinging his calloused fingers. "He doesn't call to ask about my health, Rafiq. He calls to ask about the plot size. To him, this hall isn't history. It is just three thousand square yards of commercial land waiting to be cleared."
