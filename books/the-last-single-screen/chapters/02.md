# Chapter 2: The Real Estate Shadow

By noon, the summer heat had turned the concrete lobby into an oven. The heavy ceiling fans spun with a rhythmic, metallic drone, doing little to move the thick air. The afternoon train from New Delhi station brought Kabir, Imtiaz's twenty-eight-year-old son, who stepped into the crumbling lobby looking like an alien from a different century. Dressed in a crisp linen shirt and holding a sleek leather document folder, Kabir wiped a bead of sweat from his forehead, looking up at the peeling paint of the ceiling with an expression of clinical detachment.

"Papa, I took the Metro directly from Cyber City," Kabir said, greeting his father with a brief, formal embrace. He didn't sit on the vintage wooden benches, choosing instead to lean against the solid marble pillars. "The developers are losing patience. The municipal corporation is already clearing the old warehouse behind the railway line. If we don't sign the NOC this week, they will re-route the commercial lane, and the valuation of this property will drop by thirty percent."

Imtiaz didn't open the folder Kabir placed on the desk. He stared at his son's smooth, unblemished hands—hands that had never changed a massive carbon-arc rod or scrubbed floor wax off a grand staircase.

"Your grandfather built this foundation with his own hands during the partition, Kabir," Imtiaz said, his voice dropping into a low, rumbling register. "When the city was burning, people came inside this hall to forget their hunger for three hours. You want me to let a bulldozer tear down his name so someone can open another franchise clothing store?"

"The world has moved on, Papa!" Kabir argued, his tone rising with genuine frustration. "People don't want to watch movies in a hot hall with five hundred strangers and a broken sound system. They have streaming apps on their phones. They have multiplexes with air conditioning. You are keeping a corpse on a ventilator because you are afraid of the dark."

The words hung heavily between them, cutting deeper than any legal contract ever could. Imtiaz looked down at the paper, the corporate logo of 'Vanguard Retail Spaces' gleaming back at him like a threat.
