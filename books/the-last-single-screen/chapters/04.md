# Chapter 4: The Final Screening

The announcement was written by hand on a large piece of chart paper and pasted outside the main gate: FINAL SHOW. RITZ TALKIES. ADMISSION FREE. ALL WELCOME. By 6:00 PM on Friday, an unusual energy began to hum through the narrow alleys of Daryaganj. Word had spread through the local markets, the tea stalls, and the old residential quarters.

They started arriving before the sun had fully set. Old rickshaw pullers who spent their days navigating the crowded lanes, market traders from Chandni Chowk, elderly men holding the hands of their young grandsons, and street vendors who had known Imtiaz for decades. The broken marble lobby filled up with a loud, chaotic, and vibrant human warmth it hadn't seen in over fifteen years.

Kabir stood in the corner of the lobby, his hands in his pockets, watching the crowd with an expression of profound confusion. He saw men who earned barely three hundred rupees a day dressing in their cleanest clothes just to sit in his father's crumbling auditorium.

Up in the box, Santosh struck the carbon rods together. A brilliant, blinding white hiss of light erupted inside the projector housing. The heavy cooling fans roared to life with a deep, mechanical growl. Down in the hall, the massive velvet curtains—faded and patched with age—slowly parted as the house lights dimmed to complete darkness.

A collective cheer went up from the five hundred people packed into the stalls as a classic 1970s melody exploded through the old horn speakers behind the screen. The light cutting through the dust motes in the air looked like a solid pillar of silver, connecting the tiny projection window to the giant, silver canvas. For three hours, the heat, the debt, the real estate contracts, and the changing world outside ceased to exist. The old theatre was alive, breathing its last, beautiful breath of light and sound.
