# Two Sons of Nandgaon

For forty years, one name had been enough to win Nandgaon: Tandon. Old Ram Tandon had held the parliamentary seat since before his sons could walk, building a dynasty out of canal water, cement contracts, and a genuine, if selectively applied, talent for remembering the names of a hundred thousand constituents.

When Ram Tandon suffered a stroke three months before the general election, the question of who would inherit the seat — and the party unit he had built, the Nandgaon wing of the Bharat Nirman Sena — fell to his two sons, and it split the family in a way forty years of politics never had.

Vikram, the elder, was fifty-one, had run the family's cement business for two decades, and believed the seat was his by simple seniority. He was steady, unglamorous, and trusted by the district's contractors, who liked predictability more than charisma.

Karan, the younger by nine years, had spent a decade in Delhi doing something in 'strategic communications' that nobody in the family fully understood, and had returned eighteen months earlier with a social media following, a taste for combative television appearances, and the quiet backing of the Bharat Nirman Sena's national leadership, who found him more useful on camera than his brother.

Their father, half-paralyzed and unable to speak clearly, could only watch from his hospital bed as the district unit split into two camps almost overnight. Vikram controlled the contractors, the local shopkeepers' association, and — crucially — the party's district treasurer, a Tandon loyalist named Bhargava who had personally counted votes for Ram since 1994. Karan controlled the party's state general secretary, three television anchors who owed him favors, and, more quietly, a WhatsApp network of forty thousand young voters that Vikram didn't know existed until it was too late to build his own.

The national leadership, unwilling to publicly referee a family feud that risked losing them the seat altogether, tried to broker a compromise: Vikram would get the ticket this election; Karan would be made state youth wing president, with an understanding — nothing in writing — that he'd inherit the seat in five years. Karan agreed to this in a meeting in Delhi, shook his brother's hand for the cameras, and then, within a week, had three of his own supporters file independent nominations in the Nandgaon assembly segments that fed the parliamentary seat — nominally unaffiliated, but everyone in the district understood whose blessing they carried.

Vikram confronted him at their father's house, in the room where the old man lay listening to every word without being able to answer it. 'You shook my hand in Delhi.'

'I did,' Karan said. 'And I'm not contesting against you. My people are contesting the assembly seats, which is a different election, which the party constitution allows.'

'You know exactly what you're doing.'

'I know exactly what I'm doing,' Karan agreed, without apology. 'In five years, when it's my turn, I want my own assembly MLAs already in place, not yours. You taught me that yourself, actually — you just taught it to me about cement contracts, not seats.'

Vikram won the parliamentary seat that year, comfortably, on the strength of the Tandon name and Bhargava's meticulous booth management. But two of the three assembly seats in his constituency went to Karan's independents, who were quietly readmitted to the Bharat Nirman Sena within a month of winning — a formality nobody in the state leadership even pretended to resist.

Ram Tandon died the following spring, in his sleep, having never spoken a full sentence since his stroke. At the memorial, Vikram and Karan sat together on the same stage, garlands around both their necks, each thanking their father for teaching them everything they knew. Neither mentioned that they had spent his final year on earth quietly building the armies with which they would, eventually, replace each other.

The five years of the unwritten understanding passed more tensely than either brother had expected. Vikram governed cautiously, protecting the cement contractors and shopkeepers who had put him in office, while Karan built his youth wing into something closer to a parallel party — training camps for booth workers, a media cell that produced slicker content than the state unit's own, and a growing list of young sarpanches across the district who owed their local victories to Karan's WhatsApp network rather than to Vikram's older machine.

When the moment came to honor the handover, Vikram discovered what Karan had long suspected he would: that stepping back from a seat was far harder in practice than it had sounded in principle five years earlier, especially once a man had built his own patronage inside it — a nephew placed in the district cooperative, a son-in-law awarded a fair-price shop license, small debts of loyalty that had accumulated the way debts always do in Nandgaon politics, quietly and then all at once.

Karan did not force the issue publicly. He simply let it be known, through the same television anchors who had carried his name for five years, that the party's national leadership 'remembered its commitments,' a phrase vague enough to deny and pointed enough that Vikram's own district workers understood exactly what it meant. Within a month, two of Vikram's most reliable block presidents quietly began attending Karan's youth wing meetings instead of his.

The brothers met one final time before the ticket was decided, not at their father's house this time — sold, by mutual and surprisingly amicable agreement, to fund both their campaigns — but at the cremation ground where Ram Tandon's ashes had been immersed five years before. It was Karan who chose the location, and Vikram who understood, without needing it explained, exactly what his brother was reminding him of.

'You said it yourself, five years ago,' Karan told him. 'A different election, the party constitution allows it. I'm not asking you to hand me anything. I'm telling you I already have the numbers, the same way you had the Tandon name when you needed it.'

Vikram, older now, heavier, and considerably wealthier from five years of cement contracts that a sitting MP's brother naturally attracted, found that he no longer wanted the fight as badly as he once had. He withdrew from the ticket three weeks before nomination, citing 'health and business commitments,' and campaigned, visibly and without obvious resentment, for his younger brother across all eleven assembly segments.

Karan won the seat their father had held for forty years with a margin larger than Vikram's own five years earlier — helped, though few outside the family ever knew it, by Vikram's cement contractors quietly directing their workers' votes exactly where the family had decided they should go. At the victory rally, Karan thanked his brother by name, called him 'the reason I ever had a base to build on,' and handed him, unprompted, the ceremonial first garland of the evening.

Vikram wore it without complaint. He had learned, in the five years since their father's death, that in Nandgaon a Tandon rarely lost anything permanently; he simply waited for the seat, or the contract, or the debt of loyalty, to come back around in a different shape, the way his brother's assembly MLAs had come back around five years before.
