# The Last Coalition

Chief Minister Ashok Deshmukh had governed the fictional state of Konkan Pradesh for eleven years by mastering a single skill: counting. Not votes exactly, though that mattered — counting men. Counting which of his forty-two coalition MLAs had a corruption case that could be reopened, which had a son wanting a medical college seat, which had simply grown bored of being ignored.

On a humid Tuesday in the monsoon session, his Finance Minister, Prakash Bhonsle, walked into his office without knocking and set a single sheet of paper on the desk: a list of nineteen MLAs from Deshmukh's own Konkan Janata Dal, plus eleven from the smaller ally, the Krishak Ekta Party, who had agreed — in principle, Bhonsle said — to defect to the opposition-backed Rashtra Nirman Morcha by Friday.

'In principle,' Deshmukh repeated. 'That means someone hasn't paid them yet.'

'Someone has started,' Bhonsle said. 'I don't know how far the payments have gone.'

Deshmukh had survived two such attempts before, in 2019 and 2023, both times by moving his MLAs to a resort in Goa faster than the other side could move money. But this time the Speaker, a Rashtra Nirman Morcha sympathizer despite his supposed neutrality, was rumored ready to accept resignation letters the moment thirty signatures arrived, collapsing the government before Deshmukh could even charter a bus.

He called an emergency meeting not of his MLAs but of his in-laws — specifically his wife's brother, Digvijay Rane, who ran the state's biggest sugar cooperative and, more usefully, half its cash economy. Rane arrived within the hour, ate two samosas, and asked only one question: 'How many need moving, and how many need paying?'

'Thirty need moving tonight. The other eleven need paying by Thursday, more than whatever the Morcha is offering.'

Rane made three calls. By 2 a.m., two buses and a convoy of Innovas were loading MLAs — some willingly, some grumbling, one memorably still in his lungi — for a flight to a resort in Port Blair, chosen specifically because no opposition legal team wanted to file an emergency petition in a Andaman court at midnight.

Bhonsle, watching the buses leave from Deshmukh's veranda, allowed himself a rare moment of doubt. 'Sir, even if we hold the numbers, is this — is this actually governing anymore?'

Deshmukh lit a cigarette he wasn't supposed to smoke since his bypass. 'Prakash, in eleven years I have built four hundred kilometers of rural road, three medical colleges, and a irrigation project that finally reached your own village. All of that required me to still be Chief Minister on Friday morning. You can have philosophy after Friday.'

Friday came. Thirty-nine of the forty-two MLAs on Rane's list stood behind Deshmukh at a press conference in Port Blair, garlanded and smiling, having each received — the opposition alleged, and nobody proved — considerations ranging from ministerial berths to sugar mill licenses. The Rashtra Nirman Morcha's coup collapsed three votes short. The Speaker, sensing which way the numbers had finally landed, discovered his neutrality again.

Deshmukh returned to the capital that evening and, before he did anything else, signed the file releasing funds for the irrigation project in Bhonsle's village — not as a reward, he told himself, but because the file had been sitting on his desk anyway. Bhonsle didn't ask which it was. He had learned, finally, not to.

The victory, though, was narrower than the press conference suggested. Of the thirty-nine MLAs who had stood behind him in Port Blair, at least a dozen had done so only because Rane's calls had reached them before the Rashtra Nirman Morcha's money had cleared into their accounts — a matter of hours, in some cases, not conviction. Deshmukh knew this the way he knew most things: by counting, and by not asking too many questions about numbers that had already resolved in his favor.

Three months later, the same instability returned in a different shape. The Krishak Ekta Party, chastened by how close its own MLAs had come to bolting, demanded a price for continued loyalty: the deputy chief ministership for its leader, Suresh Aage, a demand Deshmukh had refused for two years on the grounds that it would humiliate Bhonsle, who had held the post since the coalition's formation. Aage now had leverage he hadn't had before, and he knew it.

Deshmukh summoned Bhonsle to the same veranda where they'd watched the buses leave. 'Aage wants your chair.'

Bhonsle, who had spent eleven years learning patience from a man who had none himself, only nodded. 'Then give it to him for eighteen months. Let him discover what the finance department actually requires — the drought relief files, the pension arrears, the audit objections nobody in his party has ever had to answer for. He'll ask to trade it back before the monsoon session ends.'

It was, Deshmukh reflected, exactly the kind of quiet calculation he'd spent a career failing to teach his own son, who preferred television studios to ledgers. He agreed to Bhonsle's plan, more out of respect for its cynicism than conviction it would work.

Aage took the deputy chief ministership with visible triumph, garlanding himself for photographers outside the secretariat. Six months in, exactly as Bhonsle had predicted, he found himself signing off on politically toxic drought relief disbursements that pleased no one, chairing audit committees that exposed his own party's earlier expense claims, and fielding opposition questions in the assembly with none of Bhonsle's decade of rehearsed answers. By the ninth month, Aage was privately lobbying to return to his previous, less visible portfolio, citing 'the need to focus on farmer welfare directly,' a phrase that fooled nobody but satisfied the protocol of face-saving that Konkan Pradesh politics required.

Deshmukh restored Bhonsle to the finance portfolio without ceremony, in a minor reshuffle buried in the same gazette notification as four district collector transfers, precisely so that no journalist would think to ask why. Bhonsle, back at his old desk, allowed himself the question he'd swallowed on the veranda a year earlier.

'Sir. Was any of that — Aage's eighteen months — was that ever really about strengthening the coalition? Or was it always just to teach him a lesson?'

Deshmukh, older now, slower to light his forbidden cigarettes but no slower to count, considered the question longer than Bhonsle expected. 'Prakash, in this state, teaching someone a lesson and strengthening a coalition are usually the same file. You still haven't learned to stop separating them.'

He never did answer whether that was meant as criticism or as the closest thing to a compliment Ashok Deshmukh knew how to give.
