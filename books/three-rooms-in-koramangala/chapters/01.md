# Chapter One: The Flat

The three-bedroom flat on the third floor of a nondescript building in Koramangala had been, for five years, home to Ira Bhatt, Sanjana Reddy, and Meher Kapoor — three women who had met, entirely by accident, through a flat-sharing app in the chaotic weeks after each had separately moved to Bangalore for work, and who had, within the first month of living together, become something closer to family than the roommates listed on their rental agreement.

Ira was the eldest at twenty-nine, a data analyst with a dry, unhurried sense of humor and a habit of narrating her own bad decisions out loud as they happened, as though commentary alone might absolve her of consequences. Sanjana, twenty-seven, worked in marketing, moved through the world with a restless, unfiltered energy that meant she was either the loudest person in any room or, on her harder days, the quietest, with no comfortable middle setting. Meher, twenty-six, the youngest, was a UX designer, careful and observant, the one who noticed first when someone in the flat had gone quiet in a way that mattered, the one who kept, without ever discussing it as a formal system, an unofficial inventory of who needed what kind of comfort on any given night.

Their flat had, over five years, accumulated the specific archaeology of a shared life: a whiteboard in the kitchen that had started as a chore chart and evolved into a running record of inside jokes nobody could fully explain to outsiders; a single, battered rice cooker that had survived three moves within the same building as their lease terms shifted; a small shelf by the door crowded with each other's forgotten umbrellas, borrowed and never quite returned, a running joke about ownership that none of them had ever bothered to resolve because it didn't actually matter whose umbrella was whose anymore.

They had built, without any of them setting out to build it, the specific, unglamorous intimacy of people who have seen each other at their absolute worst — through breakups conducted in the kitchen at 2 a.m. with ice cream eaten directly from the tub, through Sanjana's spectacular, week-long meltdown before a product launch that Meher and Ira had talked her through in shifts, through a stomach bug that hit all three of them within the same forty-eight hours in year two and turned the flat into a chaotic, laughing disaster of shared Electral sachets and someone's mother's home remedies relayed over speakerphone.
