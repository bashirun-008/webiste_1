# Chapter Three: What the Illness Revealed

The eighteen months of Meher's treatment revealed things about all three women that five easy years of ordinary flatmate life had never required them to discover.

It revealed that Ira, whose dry humor had always functioned as a kind of protective distance, was capable of a specific, fierce tenderness she'd rarely shown anyone — sitting through entire chemotherapy infusions doing nothing but reading aloud from whatever ridiculous novel Meher was currently obsessed with, because Meher had once mentioned, offhand, that her mother used to read to her as a child when she was sick, and Ira had filed that detail away and quietly turned it into ritual.

It revealed that Sanjana's restless energy, which had sometimes read as flightiness in the easier years, was in fact a form of devotion that simply needed a crisis to properly organize itself around — she became, over eighteen months, the flat's fierce, unrelenting advocate, the one who argued with insurance companies on hold for ninety minutes at a stretch, who researched second opinions and clinical trial options with the thoroughness of someone writing a thesis, who never once, in eighteen months, let Meher see her cry, saving that for the shower, alone, where Ira eventually discovered her doing it and simply climbed in fully clothed to hold her rather than leave her to it.

It revealed, too, things about Meher herself — a specific, stubborn dignity in how she chose to face the illness, refusing, even at her sickest, to let it consume every conversation in the flat, insisting on ordinary evenings watching bad reality television with her friends, on being asked about work gossip and Sanjana's chaotic dating life and Ira's latest data-analysis complaint, needing, desperately, to still be a full person in that flat rather than only a patient.

The treatment, eighteen months in, appeared to work. A scan came back clear. The three of them cried together in the same small consultation room where they'd once received the original diagnosis, and for four months, life in the Koramangala flat returned, cautiously, gratefully, to something resembling its old rhythm — the whiteboard filled again with jokes instead of medication schedules, the rice cooker back in daily use, Meher's hair growing back in soft, defiant tufts that she took a strange, fierce pride in.
