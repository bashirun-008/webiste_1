# Chapter Six: The Two Rooms

The flat, in the months after, became a strange, aching space — three bedrooms now holding only two people, Meher's room left largely untouched for longer than either Ira or Sanjana could explain even to each other, both of them, separately, unable to be the one who suggested clearing it out.

It was Sanjana, eventually, four months in, who broached it. "I think we need to do something with her room. Not erase her. Just — stop treating it like a shrine we're both too scared to walk into."

They did it together, over a single long weekend, sorting through five years of accumulated life with the specific, careful tenderness of people handling something sacred — Meher's design sketchbooks, which Sanjana insisted on keeping intact rather than discarding, eventually mailing a selection to Meher's mother in Lucknow along with a long letter about the specific, quiet talent Meher had brought to every project she'd worked on; a box of letters from a college boyfriend that made both women cry and laugh in equal measure; the specific, ordinary detritus of an ordinary life that had also, unmistakably, been extraordinary to the two people who'd shared a flat with it for five years.

They kept the room, in the end, not as a shrine but as a guest room — repainted, refurnished, but with one small shelf left exactly as Meher had arranged it, a handful of her design awards and a photograph of the three of them at that long-delayed Goa trip, the last real vacation the three of them ever took together, all three laughing at something none of them could later remember the joke of, only the specific, complete joy of the moment itself.
