# Chapter Two: The Diagnosis

It was Meher who found the lump, in the shower, on an ordinary Tuesday in March of their fifth year living together, and Meher who, after three days of telling herself it was nothing, finally mentioned it to Ira over dinner in the specific, deliberately casual tone people use when they need someone else to either confirm their fear is silly or force them to take it seriously.

Ira did not let it be silly. She had Meher at a doctor's clinic within two days, sat with her through the ultrasound, and was the one holding Meher's hand — Sanjana on the other side, having left a client meeting the moment Ira's message reached her — when the oncologist used the words "stage two" and "aggressive" in the same sentence, three weeks later, in a small consultation room that none of the three of them would ever be able to walk past again without their chest tightening.

Meher was twenty-six years old.

The months that followed reorganized the flat's entire rhythm around chemotherapy schedules, around the specific, exhausting choreography of caring for someone through a treatment that took as much from her as it gave back. Sanjana, who had never once in five years shown any particular aptitude for domestic competence, taught herself to cook the bland, specific foods Meher could keep down after treatment, researching recipes at midnight with the same restless intensity she usually reserved for work crises. Ira rearranged her job to remote wherever possible, sitting through video calls with a laptop balanced on her knees in hospital waiting rooms, learning the specific vocabulary of oncology — CBC counts, neutrophil levels, the particular anxious math of counting days until Meher's immune system recovered enough for the next cycle.

Meher's own family, from Lucknow, offered to come stay, and did, for stretches — but it was Ira and Sanjana who were there for the ordinary, unglamorous 3 a.m. moments, the nights Meher was too nauseous to sleep and needed someone simply sitting beside her, not fixing anything, just present.

"You two didn't sign up for this," Meher said once, during a particularly hard week, her hair beginning to thin in the specific, devastating way that made the illness suddenly, visually undeniable. "You signed a flat-share agreement. This is not what roommates are supposed to do."

"We didn't sign up to be your roommates either," Sanjana said, fierce in the particular way grief made her fierce rather than soft. "We ended up being your family. Nobody signs up for family. You just end up stuck with them, and if you're very lucky, it turns out you'd have chosen them anyway."
