# Chapter Seven: What Grief Leaves Behind

Ira and Sanjana stayed in the flat together for two more years before life's ordinary momentum eventually moved them, separately, into new chapters — Ira to a promotion that required relocating to Mumbai, Sanjana, eventually, into a flat of her own a few kilometers away, ready, finally, for a space that wasn't organized around the specific, complicated grief the Koramangala flat now permanently held.

They did not drift apart the way distance sometimes allows grief-bound friendships to quietly dissolve once the acute crisis passes. If anything, the loss they'd carried together had welded something into their friendship that proved sturdier than geography — a weekly video call neither ever let slide, an annual trip to Meher's favorite beach in Goa that they'd started, without officially deciding to, treating as a permanent ritual, a standing tradition of ordering that same specific biryani on Meher's birthday every year, eating it together, wherever they happened to be, over a video call if they couldn't manage in person.

"Do you think we'd have become this close without her?" Sanjana asked once, years later, the two of them on their annual Goa trip, sitting on the same stretch of beach where they'd once brought Meher for her last good vacation.

Ira considered this for a long moment, watching the waves in the specific, unhurried way she'd learned, somewhere in the last few years, to actually sit still with hard questions instead of deflecting them with humor. "I think we'd have been friends regardless. I don't think we'd have been *this*, though. I think some kinds of closeness only get built by going through something that costs you everything together. I hate that it took losing her to get here. But I don't think either of us would trade what we became, even knowing the cost."

"She said that, near the end," Sanjana said quietly. "That she got to be loved like that. I think about it all the time. I think we got something too. Not the same thing — obviously not the same thing, she's the one who died, not us. But we got to love someone all the way through the worst thing that can happen to a person, and come out the other side still standing, still each other's family. That's not nothing either."

Somewhere behind them, back in Koramangala, three rooms still held the specific, permanent architecture of a friendship that illness had tested and grief had reshaped but never once managed to break — a whiteboard, long since repurposed for new jokes, that still, if you looked closely enough at one corner nobody had ever quite had the heart to erase completely, bore the faint ghost of Meher's handwriting: *Unfinished Business.*

Some of it, they both understood now, would simply always stay unfinished. That, too, they had learned to carry, together, as the specific, ordinary cost of having loved someone all the way to the end.
