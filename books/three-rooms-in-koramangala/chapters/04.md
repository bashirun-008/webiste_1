# Chapter Four: The Return

The cancer came back in month five of remission, in Meher's liver this time, discovered during what was supposed to be a routine follow-up scan, and the specific vocabulary the three of them had learned to dread — "metastatic," "aggressive," "limited options" — returned to the small consultation room with a finality that none of them, despite everything they'd already survived together, were prepared for.

Meher was twenty-eight years old when the oncologist, gently but without evasion, told her the treatment options remaining were about extending time rather than achieving remission. She asked, in the quiet after the doctor left the room, for a specific number, and was given one — a range, months rather than years, delivered with the careful, practiced compassion of a doctor who had delivered this particular sentence too many times to make it easy but not so many times that it had become routine.

Ira and Sanjana did not, in the months that followed, fall apart in front of Meher, though both, separately, fell apart plenty in private — Ira in the specific, controlled way she processed everything, alone on the flat's small balcony at 2 a.m. with a cup of tea going cold in her hands; Sanjana in the loud, physical way grief moved through her, a run around the neighborhood park at dawn that some mornings ended in her simply sitting on a bench, unable to move for twenty minutes, before pulling herself together enough to come home and make Meher's breakfast.

What they did, together, in front of Meher, was build the specific, deliberate kindness of the time that remained. They took her, on a good week, to Goa for four days, the three of them on a beach they'd talked about visiting together since the first year of the flat-share and never quite managed to schedule. They filled the whiteboard, at Meher's specific request, not with jokes this time but with a list she called, with the particular dark humor illness sometimes grants people, "Unfinished Business" — small, specific things she wanted before the end, most of them absurdly modest: a proper biryani from a specific restaurant across town, one more viewing of her favorite childhood film with both of them beside her, a night where all three of them got, deliberately, mildly drunk and told each other every embarrassing secret they'd never admitted in five years of living together.
