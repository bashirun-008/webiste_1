# Chapter Five: The Last Good Months

The months that followed were the hardest and, in a way neither Ira nor Sanjana would ever be able to fully explain to anyone who hadn't lived through it, also some of the most tender of their entire friendship.

Meher, weaker now, spent most days on the flat's worn living room sofa, and the two of them reorganized their entire lives around simply being present in that room with her — Ira working from the armchair beside the sofa instead of her desk, Sanjana taking calls from the kitchen so she'd never be more than a room away. They watched, at Meher's insistence, an absurd amount of trashy reality television, the three of them narrating contestants' terrible decisions with the same running commentary they'd developed over five ordinary years, because Meher had been firm, early on, that she did not want her final months to become a solemn vigil — she wanted, as much as possible, to still be simply living in the flat with her friends, laughing at stupid television, rather than performing the role of a dying woman for an audience of two.

There was one night, near the end, when Meher, lucid and calm in a way that felt, to both Ira and Sanjana, like its own kind of gift, asked them both to sit on either side of her on the sofa, the way they had that first week of the diagnosis, and said, quietly, "I need you to know something, and I need you to actually hear it, not just be kind about it. Getting sick was the worst thing that ever happened to me. But living in this flat, with you two, for these five years — sick or not — has been the best thing. I wouldn't trade it. Not the diagnosis, obviously. But everything around it. I got to be loved like this. Not everyone gets that. I want you to remember that I know how lucky I was, even now."

Neither Ira nor Sanjana could speak for a long moment. It was Ira, eventually, who managed, "You gave us that too, Meher. This wasn't just something that happened to you that we showed up for. You made us better at loving people. Both of us. I don't think either of us knew we had this in us before you."

Meher passed six weeks later, at home in the flat rather than a hospital — her specific, stubborn request, granted through a hospice arrangement Sanjana had spent weeks organizing with the same relentless thoroughness she'd once brought to insurance calls — with Ira holding one hand and Sanjana the other, the whiteboard in the kitchen still bearing, in Meher's own handwriting from a better week, the last unfinished item on her list, crossed out three days earlier when Sanjana had finally tracked down that specific biryani.
