# Chapter Five: What the Team Gave Each Kid

The tournament itself, when it finally arrived, was almost secondary to what four months of serious, structured training had already given each of the eight kids individually, in ways that had little to do with cricket's actual scoreboard.

Anjali's father, informed by his daughter herself — with a directness she'd found, somewhere in the training, that she hadn't previously possessed — that she had been playing cricket for two years and intended to keep playing regardless of his approval, was so startled by his usually quiet daughter's newfound conviction that he came, reluctantly, to watch a practice match, and left, several weeks later, having become, however grudgingly, the team's most reliable spectator, standing at the boundary of the dusty Sector 12 ground shouting encouragement he'd once have considered beneath his dignity to offer a daughter playing "an unsuitable sport."

Sunny's mother, watching her son's report card remain, exactly as Ranbir had promised, entirely undiminished by four months of serious cricket commitment, found herself, by the tournament's approach, quietly proud of a son whose growing confidence on the field had begun, unexpectedly, to bleed into a new confidence in his classroom presentations, a shift his teachers noticed and remarked on before his own mother fully connected it to the cricket she'd once feared was a distraction.

Deepak, whose rebuilt bowling action had cost him weeks of frustration, found, in the tournament's opening match, that the technically sound yorker Ranbir had painstakingly reconstructed produced results his old, unpolished instinct never could have managed consistently — three wickets in a single spell against a genuinely strong opposing team, a result that did more for Deepak's fragile confidence than any single conversation about self-worth ever could have.

Vicky, whose entire identity had been built around being the loud, unstoppable captain holding a ragtag group together through sheer force of personality, discovered, over the tournament's stressful middle rounds, a genuine talent for tactical thinking under pressure that surprised even Ranbir, calling field placements and bowling changes with an instinct that suggested, Ranbir told him privately, "the makings of a real captain, not just a loud one, which believe me, are not the same thing."
