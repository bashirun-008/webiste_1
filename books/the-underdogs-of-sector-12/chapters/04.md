# Chapter Four: The Tournament

The local Sector cricket tournament — an annual, modestly organized affair between neighborhood teams across Chandigarh's various residential sectors, taken seriously by the participants and almost entirely ignored by everyone else — became, over the following four months of Ranbir's increasingly committed coaching, the specific, concrete goal around which the Underdogs' entire summer organized itself.

Ranbir, whatever bitterness he still carried about his own career's unjust ending, coached the eight kids with a rigor and technical precision he'd never once brought to his joyless work at the private academy, staying late most evenings well past his contracted commitment, using his own modest salary, more than once, to buy proper cricket gear for kids whose families couldn't afford it — a pair of proper batting gloves for Sunny that arrived with no explanation beyond "found these, thought they might fit," a decent pair of cricket shoes for Anjali that Ranbir presented with the specific gruffness of a man uncomfortable being caught in an act of generosity.

The training was not without conflict. Deepak, frustrated by weeks of having his cherished yorker technique dismantled and rebuilt from the ground up, nearly quit twice, convinced Ranbir was destroying the one thing that made him special rather than improving it. Sunny's mother, discovering the extent of her son's commitment to the training, arrived at the ground one evening, furious, demanding to know why her son was wasting hours he should be spending on his studies on a sport that, in her considered, exhausted experience, had never once lifted anyone out of a life like theirs.

It was Ranbir, unexpectedly, who talked her through it — not by dismissing her fears, which he understood, with the specific weight of his own unjust career, better than most, but by making her a promise: that he would personally ensure Sunny's studies never slipped below a standard she approved of, checking report cards himself if necessary, treating the cricket as something that had to earn its place alongside her son's education rather than instead of it.

"I know what this sport can take from a person if it doesn't work out," Ranbir told her, quiet and direct. "I know better than almost anyone. I'm not going to let it take anything from Sunny that he can't afford to lose. But I've also watched him bat for four months now, and I think it would be a genuine waste to let fear of what might not work out stop him from finding out what he's actually capable of."
