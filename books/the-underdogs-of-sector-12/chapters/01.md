# Chapter One: The Ground

The cricket ground behind Sector 12 in Chandigarh was not, by any reasonable definition, a cricket ground — a dusty rectangle of packed earth wedged between a row of government-quota housing blocks and a defunct municipal water tank, its boundary marked by nothing more than an unofficial agreement among the neighborhood kids about where the tank's shadow fell in the evening.

It was here that seven boys and one girl, aged between ten and fourteen, had been playing an increasingly serious, increasingly organized version of gully cricket for two years, under no coach, no formal structure, and no expectation from anyone in Sector 12 — a neighborhood of auto drivers, daily-wage laborers, and government clerks two pay grades from comfortable — that any of them would amount to much beyond the specific, limited horizons their parents had already resigned themselves to.

The team, if it could be called that, had no official name until Vicky Thakur, the group's twelve-year-old self-appointed captain — loud, undersized, and possessed of a batting technique built entirely out of instinct rather than instruction — declared, after a particularly humiliating defeat to a well-funded private school team during an informal challenge match, that they would henceforth be known as "the Underdogs," a name meant, in Vicky's own words, "so everyone already knows we're going to surprise them."

The other seven members ranged widely. Deepak, fourteen, the group's fastest bowler, worked mornings helping his father's vegetable cart before school and had a natural, un-coached yorker that could have belonged to a state-level player if anyone had ever bothered to properly train it. Sunny, thirteen, was the group's most technically gifted batsman, quiet and serious, the son of a widowed mother who cleaned houses across three different sectors and who worried, constantly, that cricket was a distraction from the studies that represented her son's only realistic path out of the life she'd had. Anjali, eleven, the only girl in the group and, by unanimous informal agreement, its best fielder, played despite a father who considered cricket "not suitable for a girl" and had no idea, each evening, that his daughter was sneaking out to the ground rather than, as she'd told him, going to a friend's house to study.
