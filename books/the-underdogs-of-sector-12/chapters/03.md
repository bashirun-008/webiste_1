# Chapter Three: What They Were Missing

The trial session revealed to Ranbir, within the first hour, exactly how much raw ability the Sector 12 kids had accumulated with essentially no proper instruction — and exactly how many basic, correctable flaws stood between that raw ability and anything resembling real competitive cricket. Sunny's technically gifted batting collapsed against anything faster than gully-cricket pace because nobody had ever taught him to properly watch the ball out of the bowler's hand rather than guessing at trajectory. Deepak's dangerous yorker was undermined by a run-up so inconsistent it telegraphed the delivery to any batsman paying attention. Anjali's excellent fielding instincts were paired with a throwing technique that would eventually damage her shoulder if nobody corrected it.

Ranbir found himself, against every bitter instinct fifteen years of resentment had built in him, unable to walk away from what he was seeing — not just talent, though there was plenty of that, but a specific, hungry seriousness about the game that the well-funded academy kids, cushioned by parents who'd bought them into the sport rather than kids who'd fought their way toward it through sheer stubborn love, rarely displayed.

"I'll do two sessions a week," he told Vicky, at the end of that first trial, with the specific, grudging tone of a man surprising himself. "No promises beyond that. And you're going to hate me within a month, because I'm going to make you rebuild half of what you think you already know how to do."

"We already hate everyone who beats us," Vicky said. "At least this way we'll hate someone who's helping."
