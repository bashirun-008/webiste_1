# Chapter 1: K-Pop and Kitchen Smoke

The damp fog of Mokokchung didn't roll into the hills; it claimed them, hanging thick over the sloping corrugated zinc roofs and softening the sharp edges of the pine trees. Inside her bedroom, nineteen-year-old Arenla stood balanced on the tips of her worn white sneakers, her eyes fixed entirely on the cracked screen of her smartphone leaned against a stack of textbooks. From the tinny speakers, a heavy, synchronized synth-bass beat filled the small room—the latest comeback track from a breakout South Korean pop group.

She replicated the choreography with obsessive precision. Step, pivot, snap the wrists, shift the weight back. Her movements were sharp, athletic, and modern, completely out of sync with the environment around her. Through the floorboards, the low, rhythmic crackle of wood fires drifted up from the kitchen below, carrying with it the unmistakable, rich smell of smoked pork cooking slowly over oak embers.

"Arenla! The water is boiling! Come down before your uncle arrives!" her grandmother's voice cut through the music, carrying the raspy weight of a lifetime spent in the hills.

Arenla paused the video mid-frame, catching her breath as she looked at her reflection in the small vanity mirror. Her cheeks were flushed, her dyed copper-streaked hair tied back in a messy bun. She adjusted her oversized black graphic hoodie—bought from an online thrift store based out of Dimapur—and muted the phone. To her grandmother, the music was nothing but "senseless noise from the television nations," but to Arenla, the slick digital world of Seoul felt closer and infinitely more reachable than the capital city of New Delhi, which existed only as a sterile chapter in her political science textbooks.

She hurried down the steep wooden steps into the dark, smoke-stained kitchen. Her grandmother, Azungla, sat on a low wooden stool by the hearth, her weathered fingers effortlessly turning pieces of dried meat over the iron grill. The walls were blackened by decades of pine smoke, a stark contrast to the brilliant, saturated blues and pinks of the digital landscape Arenla had just stepped out of.

"Your mind is always wandering across the ocean, Aren," Azungla murmured without looking up, her voice surprisingly gentle despite her stern posture. "The world doesn't live inside that small plastic box. Sit. Eat some rice. Your hands are cold."
