# Chapter 2: The Indigo Dye

The next morning brought a rare break in the monsoon clouds, casting a pale, liquid gold light across the veranda. Azungla had cleared the center of the wooden floor, laying out a heavy bundle of raw, hand-spun cotton threads and several clay pots containing a deep, dark indigo paste fermented from wild forest leaves. Today was the day she intended to pass down the traditional weaving patterns of the Ao Naga tribe—a heritage meticulously preserved not in books, but in the memory of fingers.

"Every stripe has a name, and every name has a story, Aren," Azungla explained, dipping her stained hands into the indigo paste. Her fingers were permanently dyed a dark blue-grey, looking like aged flint. "This pattern belongs to our clan. When you wear this shawl, people down in the plains or up in the high villages will know exactly whose blood runs in your veins. They will know you are not lost."

Arenla sat cross-legged opposite her grandmother, trying to follow the complex cross-weaving matrix. She respected the craft—she truly did—but her pocket kept vibrating with relentless intensity. Every buzz was a notification from a WhatsApp group chat titled 'Seoul-Bound 2026'. A major South Korean entertainment agency had announced its first-ever digital global audition specifically targeting youth from Northeast India, recognizing the immense, untapped subculture thriving in cities like Kohima, Imphal, and Shillong.

She watched her grandmother's hands dance across the crude wooden loom. The rhythm was hypnotic: clack, slide, press. Clack, slide, press. It was a beautiful, ancient loop. Yet, Arenla couldn't help but compare it to the digital grids of the video editing apps she used to clip her dance covers. Both required absolute structural logic, but one bound her to the soil of Mokokchung forever, while the other offered a faint, glittering passport out of it.

"Are you listening to me, child?" Azungla asked, noticing Arenla's eyes tracking a stray beam of light on the floorboards.

"I am listening, Aba," Arenla said softly, taking a damp strand of blue thread. "But what if the world doesn't care about our stripes anymore? What if the people outside only see us as a small dot on the edge of the map?"

Azungla paused, her blue-stained hands resting on the rough timber of the loom. "Let them see a dot, Aren. But a dot is where a line begins."
