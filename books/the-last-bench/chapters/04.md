# Chapter Four: The Slow Fade

What happened next was not a dramatic falling-out, which was, in its own way, worse — a rupture might have at least given both boys something to point to, some specific wound to eventually heal from. Instead, the letters simply slowed, the way things do when two teenagers are each independently trying to build entirely new lives in entirely new cities, surrounded by entirely new people who don't require the effort of a postal service to maintain.

Rahul, in Pune, threw himself into a new school where nobody knew him as anything except the quiet new boy, and found, to his own surprise, that reinventing himself as slightly less quiet was easier in a place with no history to contradict him. Farhan, in Nagpur, found his social world reshuffling around Rahul's absence in the specific way a friend group does when its center of gravity moves away — new friendships forming on the last bench of Row C, a bench Farhan eventually, guiltily, stopped sitting in entirely because it felt wrong to occupy it with anyone else.

The letters that had started weekly became monthly, then, by the time both boys reached Class 12 and the genuine chaos of board exams and college applications consumed every spare hour, stopped almost entirely — not from any decision either boy made, but from the specific, unremarkable erosion that distance performs on even the strongest friendships when neither person quite notices the exact letter that turns out to be the last one.

They did not, in the end, apply to the same engineering college. Rahul went to a college in Mumbai; Farhan, whose grades had never been as strong as Rahul's careful diligence, ended up at a smaller college in Nagpur itself. They exchanged a handful of messages over the following decade — a birthday wish here, a "congratulations" when Rahul's engagement was announced on Facebook, a brief, awkward phone call when Farhan's father passed away that neither boy quite knew how to navigate, both painfully aware of how much unspoken distance now sat between two people who had once known everything about each other.

By the time both boys turned thirty, the friendship that had defined their entire childhood had become something closer to a fond, aching memory than an active relationship — the specific, particular grief of losing someone who technically hadn't died, hadn't even really fought with you, had simply drifted, the way water drifts, until the gap became too wide to easily cross without acknowledging, painfully, how long it had been allowed to grow.
