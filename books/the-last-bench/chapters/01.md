# Chapter One: Two Boys, One Bench

Rahul Deshpande and Farhan Sheikh became best friends on the second day of Class 3, for the deeply unserious reason that they were both assigned to the last bench of Row C at St. Xavier's School in Nagpur, a placement handed down by a teacher who arranged children alphabetically by surname and had no idea she was setting in motion a friendship that would define both boys' childhoods.

They were, on paper, an unlikely match. Rahul was quiet, careful, the kind of boy who finished his homework the day it was assigned and worried, genuinely, about disappointing teachers who barely registered his existence. Farhan was loud, restless, perpetually in minor trouble for talking during class, drawing cartoons in the margins of his notebooks instead of taking notes, the kind of boy teachers noticed immediately and usually for the wrong reasons.

What they shared, it turned out, was a specific, complete lack of interest in being anyone other than exactly who they were, and a sense of humor that clicked, instantly and completely, in the particular way that only happens a handful of times in a person's whole life. By the end of that second day, they had already invented a private language of inside jokes — a specific way of saying "boring" that involved rolling their eyes back exaggeratedly, a running joke about the school's ancient, terrifying peon Bhaskar Kaka that would sustain them for a full decade — and had claimed the last bench of Row C as, unofficially but permanently, theirs.

They stayed on that bench, by choice, for the next nine years, through six different teachers who tried and failed to separate them, through every school trip, every cricket match played in the dusty ground behind the school, every ordinary, golden, unremarkable afternoon of a childhood neither of them, at the time, understood they would spend the rest of their lives measuring everything else against.
