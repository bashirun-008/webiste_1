# Chapter Six: The Reunion

The tenth-year school reunion for St. Xavier's Class of that particular year was organized, with the specific relentless energy of alumni committees everywhere, by a batchmate neither Rahul nor Farhan had been especially close to, and both men, independently, nearly didn't go — Rahul citing work, Farhan citing the specific discomfort of returning to a school that held more memory of Rahul than of anyone who'd actually stayed.

They both went anyway, in the end, pulled by the same vague, unexamined pull that draws most people back to reunions: a curiosity about who everyone had become, wrapped around a grief neither would have named even to himself.

They saw each other across the school's decorated hall — the same hall where their Class 10 farewell had been held thirteen years earlier — and for a long moment, neither moved, both of them, in that instant, exactly seventeen again, standing on the edge of a countdown neither had known how to properly survive.

"You got old," Farhan said finally, crossing the room, and something in the specific, familiar rhythm of the insult — exactly the kind of thing seventeen-year-old Farhan would have said — broke something open in both of them at once.

"You got taller," Rahul said, which wasn't remotely true, and didn't need to be.

They ended up, an hour into the reunion, walking the school grounds alone, past the cricket pitch, past the classroom that had once housed the last bench of Row C, now repainted, refurnished, unrecognizable except in the specific way memory refuses to let a place be fully replaced by whatever's currently standing there.

"I wrote you a letter," Rahul said, somewhere near the old classroom door, the confession arriving before he'd fully decided to make it. "First year of college. I never sent it. I've had it in a drawer for thirteen years."

Farhan stopped walking. "I wrote you one too," he said slowly. "After my father died. I never sent it either."

They stood there, in the doorway of a classroom that no longer held the specific bench that had once held an entire childhood, and both, without needing to discuss it further, understood exactly what the other's unsent letter had cost them both to carry alone for over a decade.
