# Chapter Three: The Last Year

The trouble, when it came, arrived in the unglamorous, bureaucratic form of a bank transfer letter. Rahul's father, a manager at a nationalized bank, was informed in the middle of Class 10 — the most important year of school, the year everything was supposed to be building toward board exams and, unspoken beneath that, toward the slow, natural process of two best friends becoming adults together in the same city — that he was being transferred to a branch in Pune, effective the following month.

The two boys had exactly six weeks between the news and the move, and they spent it in the specific, desperate way teenagers try to compress an entire remaining future into a countdown they don't yet have the emotional tools to properly process.

They made promises the way seventeen-year-olds always do — enormous, sincere, and structurally unequipped to survive the actual mechanics of distance. They would call every week. They would visit every school holiday. They would, Farhan declared with total conviction one evening on the last bench, both apply to the same engineering college regardless of where either of their families ended up, so the friendship would simply continue, unbroken, into the next chapter exactly as it had always been.

On Rahul's last day in Nagpur, the two of them sat on the last bench of Row C — technically no longer their assigned seats, both boys having been moved around by different teachers over the years, but the bench that had remained, in every meaningful sense, theirs — long after the school day had ended, until the peon Bhaskar Kaka, the same terrifying figure they'd joked about for nine years, came to lock up the classroom and found, instead of scolding them, something in their faces that made him simply say, gruffly, "Five more minutes," and walk away.

"I'm going to write to you every week," Farhan said. "Actual letters. Not just calls. Something you can keep."

"I'll write back every time," Rahul promised.

They did, for the first four months.
