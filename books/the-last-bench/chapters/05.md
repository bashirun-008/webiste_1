# Chapter Five: The Letter Neither Sent

What neither of them knew — what neither would learn until years later, at the reunion that finally brought them back together — was that each had, independently, written one last letter during the worst of the drift, in their early twenties, during a period each was privately struggling through, and neither had ever sent it.

Rahul had written his during a bruising first year of engineering college, homesick and friendless in a hostel in Mumbai, missing the specific, easy comfort of a friendship that had never once required him to perform confidence he didn't feel. He had written four pages, raw and honest in a way his careful, controlled adult self would later be faintly embarrassed by, describing exactly how lost he felt, how much he missed the last bench, how he didn't know how to say any of this out loud to anyone in his new life who hadn't known him since he was eight years old. He had folded the letter, addressed the envelope, and then, gripped by a sudden, specific fear that too much time had passed for a confession this vulnerable to land as anything but strange, had put it in a drawer instead of a postbox, telling himself he'd send it once he felt less pathetic. He never did.

Farhan had written his during the year after his father's death, a letter he'd started a dozen times and abandoned, trying and failing to explain to the one person who had once known his family well enough to understand exactly what the loss meant, how alone he felt navigating it with friends who offered condolences but not the specific, textured understanding of someone who'd eaten dinner at that same table a hundred times as a boy. He, too, had never sent it, telling himself, in the specific self-protective logic of grief, that reaching out after so many years of silence over something this painful would seem less like reconnection and more like using an old friend as an emotional convenience.

Both letters sat, unsent, in drawers in two different cities, for years.
