# Chapter Five: The Wedding

Kabir's wedding, when it finally arrived two days later, was the kind of joyfully chaotic affair that gave the four of them exactly the distraction and celebration they needed after a night that had cracked all of them open in ways none had expected. They danced badly, embarrassed themselves at the sangeet with a choreographed dance routine Priya had insisted on rehearsing that afternoon with a competence that fooled nobody, and cried a little, all four of them, when Kabir — spotting them from the mandap — mouthed, silently, across the crowd, *thank you for coming, chaos,* using the group chat's name like it was still, after everything, exactly who they were.

But it was the morning after the wedding, all four of them nursing hangovers on the villa's terrace with the ocean flat and silver in front of them, that the real repair happened — not through some grand gesture, but through the specific, unglamorous work of actually making promises they intended to keep.

"I want a real call," Neha said. "Not the group chat. An actual video call, once a month, where we're not allowed to just send memes and dodge real questions. I want to know what's actually happening in your lives, and I want to start telling you what's actually happening in mine."

"I'll go first every time if it makes it easier," Arjun offered. "Given I apparently need the practice."

"I'm booking a doctor's appointment the day I land," he added, before anyone could ask.

"I'm going to start actually finishing the design freelance projects I keep starting and abandoning at 11 p.m. instead of just telling myself I don't have time," Neha said. "Not because I'm going back to it full-time. Just because I miss it, and I'm allowed to miss it without that meaning I regret my life."

"I'm going to stop leaving jobs the second they start feeling real," Sameer said. "I don't fully know how yet. But I'm going to try, and I'm going to actually tell you three when I'm panicking instead of dressing it up as 'creative differences.'"

Priya, uncharacteristically, was the last to speak, and when she did, her voice wobbled in a way none of them had heard from her since a particularly brutal semester in third year. "I'm going to stop pretending the startup is easy. I'm going to stop performing 'I've got this' at all of you the same way I perform it for my investors, because you three are supposed to be the people I don't have to perform for, and somewhere in the last eight years I forgot that."
