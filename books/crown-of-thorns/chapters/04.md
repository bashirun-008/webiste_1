# Chapter Four: The Slow Climb

Noor's rise, this time, was deliberate in a way her first life's had never had the luxury of being. She let her voice be discovered gradually, singing at increasingly important gatherings until word reached the right ears at the pace of genuine reputation rather than sudden, suspicious favor. She cultivated relationships with the court's minor officials — clerks, stewards, the unglamorous administrative machinery that actually kept a palace running and that, in her first life, she had learned too late held more real intelligence about court conspiracies than any noble's drawing room.

And she kept finding reasons to be near Iskandar, not as a calculated strategy but because she discovered, with a kind of quiet astonishment, that she liked him — genuinely, uncomplicatedly, in a way that had nothing to do with the desperate, grateful loyalty she'd once felt toward him only after he'd proven himself irreplaceable during a decade of court warfare. This time she got to like him first, slowly, for ordinary reasons: his dry, understated humor that took weeks to notice because he delivered it so flatly; his stubborn honesty even when a convenient lie would have served him better; the particular gentleness with which he treated the palace's lowest-ranked servants, the stable boys and grain counters nobody else at court bothered to learn the names of.

"Why do you spend so much time with the stable staff," she asked him once, watching him help a limping groom carry feed sacks he had no obligation to touch himself.

"Because someday, when the people who currently matter to this court decide I don't matter anymore, it will be these people — not the nobles — who remember whether I treated them with decency," Iskandar said. "I'd rather have earned that memory honestly than discover too late I hadn't bothered."

Noor thought, with the particular ache of a woman who had once lost this exact man to a manufactured errand on the worst night of her life, that he had understood something about loyalty and power that most of the court's most brilliant nobles never once grasped.
