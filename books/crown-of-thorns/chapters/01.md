# Chapter One: The Fall of an Empress

Noor Jehangir died on a night of celebration, which was its own particular cruelty.

She had ruled — not by title, never by title, but in every way that mattered — for eleven years as the favored wife of an aging emperor in a fictional Hindustani court modeled on the splendor of the great Mughal age, and she had learned, in that time, to read the currents of court intrigue the way a riverboat captain reads water: which eddies were dangerous, which stillness concealed the strongest pull, which smiling face at the durbar was already planning her downfall.

She had not read her own physician carefully enough.

The coup came on the night of her stepson's wedding, timed for the hour when the court's attention was fixed on fireworks over the palace lake instead of on the empress's private chambers, where a cup of spiced wine she had trusted — because she had always trusted the boy who brought it, a servant named Fazil she had personally saved from execution years earlier — carried a poison slow enough to look, to anyone examining the body later, like a simple failure of an aging heart.

She understood, in her last clear moments, exactly who had ordered it: the coalition of nobles she had outmaneuvered for a decade, finally united by nothing more than a shared exhaustion with being outmaneuvered by a woman they had never stopped resenting for the crime of being cleverer than all of them combined.

She understood, too, in the specific bitterness of her final breath, that the one person in the entire glittering, treacherous court whose loyalty she had never once had to question — a quiet, disgraced prince named Salim's half-brother, a man named Iskandar, born to a lesser wife and given no real title, no real power, no real reason at all to serve her except that he had simply chosen to, year after year, without asking anything in return — had been sent, that very night, on a manufactured errand to the eastern border, precisely so he would not be present to protect her when the poison finally worked.

She died thinking his name, which she had never once, in eleven years, allowed herself to say aloud with the weight it deserved.
