# Chapter Three: The Disgraced Prince

Iskandar, in this earlier version of the court, was twenty years old, half-brother to the reigning prince Salim, born to a concubine of insufficient rank to ever grant him real standing, and already, at twenty, quietly resigned to a life spent at the margins of power he would never be permitted to hold.

He was not disgraced yet — that particular fall from what little standing he had would come later, in three years' time, engineered by the same coalition of nobles who would one day poison Noor, as a way of removing the one man in court whose sense of honor made him inconveniently difficult to buy. In this earlier timeline, he was simply overlooked: competent, quiet, dutiful in the unglamorous administrative postings nobody more ambitious wanted, the kind of man courtiers walked past without registering because he made no claim on their attention.

Noor found him, on her third week back in this earlier life, exactly where she remembered he would be found in any timeline: at the edge of the palace stables, checking the ledgers for the royal horses with a thoroughness that bored administrators twice his age couldn't be bothered to match.

"You count the same sacks of grain three times," she observed, leaning against the stable door. "Once when they arrive, once when the steward reports them, and once yourself, quietly, after everyone's gone."

Iskandar looked up, startled that anyone had noticed a habit he'd assumed was invisible. "The steward's numbers don't always match the grain merchant's. Someone is skimming a small amount every month. I haven't proven who yet."

"You will," Noor said, with the total, uncanny certainty of a woman who had, in another life, watched this exact quiet diligence eventually expose an embezzlement ring that had been bleeding the royal stables for a decade — one of the first, unglamorous ways Iskandar had earned her trust, back when she'd had a decade of trust to give him before losing it all in a single poisoned cup. "It's the deputy steward. Ghulam Rasool. He's been doing it for years, and when you finally catch him, he'll try to convince you it's not worth pursuing because he has powerful friends. Pursue it anyway."

Iskandar studied her for a long moment, the particular searching look of a man trying to determine whether he'd just met a very perceptive stranger or someone who knew something impossible. "How could you possibly know that?"

"I have a gift for reading patterns," Noor said, which was, in its way, the truest available version of an answer she could not fully explain.
