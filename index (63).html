Chapter Four: What Nobody Said

It was Neha, unexpectedly, who cracked first, three drinks deep and suddenly, uncharacteristically, unable to keep performing the contentment she'd been curating in every family photo for three years.

"I haven't opened my design software in two years," she said, staring at her glass rather than at any of them. "Not once. Rohit's great, my daughter is the best thing that's ever happened to me, and also I have a whole self that just quietly disappeared somewhere in the last four years and nobody — including me — noticed it was gone until right now, saying it out loud to you idiots."

"Why didn't you ever say anything?" Priya asked, gentler now.

"Because what was I going to say? 'I have a healthy marriage and a beautiful kid and I'm still sad sometimes about a career I chose to pause'? That sounds insane. That sounds ungrateful." Neha's voice cracked slightly. "So I just stopped bringing it up. Even to you three. Especially to you three, honestly, because Priya's out here building an actual company and I didn't want to sound like I was complaining about a life that looks, from the outside, like I got everything."

Arjun was quiet for a long moment, and then, in the specific way people confess things only after someone else has gone first and made it safe, said, "I've been having chest pains for four months. I haven't seen a doctor because I know it's stress and I don't want to hear a doctor tell me what I already know, which is that the job that's supposedly going so well for me is quietly killing me, and I have no idea how to fix it without blowing up a life everyone thinks is the successful one."

"Arjun," Priya said, alarmed. "You need to see a doctor. Actually see one. Not know-it's-probably-stress it."

"I know," Arjun said. "I will. I just — I haven't said that out loud to anyone either, until right now."

The four of them sat with that for a while, the sundowner rum forgotten, the ocean loud in the dark beyond the villa's patio.

"Okay," Sameer said eventually, quieter than his usual register. "Since we're doing this. The truth about the job-hopping isn't that I get bored. It's that I panic, every time a job starts feeling like it might actually work long-term, because some part of me is convinced I'm going to get exposed as not actually good enough, and it's easier to leave first than wait to be told that." He exhaled, shaky. "I've never said that to anyone either. Not my parents, not any of the situationships I made you all laugh about for four years. You three are, genuinely, the only people I've ever been honest with, and I've spent eight years barely talking to you."
