Chapter 3: The Highway Cafe

By late afternoon, Arenla had escaped the domestic enclosure of the kitchen to meet her friends at The Cloud Nine Cafe, a small, vibrant establishment perched precariously on a cliffside overlooking the state highway. The cafe was the unofficial headquarters for the town's youth—a space where acoustic guitars, imported fashion magazines, and the smell of fresh cinnamon lattes coexisted under a canopy of low-hanging mist.

Her friends, Sentiru and Toshi, were already seated by the window, sharing a plate of spicy Korean ramen while analyzing a PDF document on a laptop screen.

"The application portal closes at midnight tonight," Sentiru said, her voice a mix of anxiety and excitement. She pointed to the glowing screen. "Look at the submission guidelines. We need a high-definition video, clear lighting, and a brief introduction in English or basic Korean. Have you recorded your routine yet, Aren?"

"Not yet. My grandmother had me working on the loom all morning," Arenla sighed, dropping into a bamboo chair. "And the internet connection at my house keeps dropping every time the wind blows hard."

"Use the cafe's Wi-Fi," Toshi suggested, looking up from his guitar. He was tuning the strings to a melancholic open chord. "But seriously, Aren, what happens if you actually pass the first round? You'll have to go to Guwahati or Delhi for the live finals. Your uncle will never agree to fund that. He wants you to take the state civil service exams."

The mention of the civil service exams cast a familiar, heavy shadow over the table. In these mountains, security meant a government job—sitting in a dimly lit bureaucratic office in Kohima, filling out paperwork for thirty years. It was the safe path, the honorable path, the path that made parents proud. But to Arenla, it felt like an extended sentence in a concrete cage.

"I don't want to spend my life preparing for an exam that three lakh people are fighting for," Arenla said, her voice hardening with sudden conviction. "I want to move. I want to feel like my body belongs to something I chose, not just something I inherited."
