Chapter Seven: The Night That Doesn't Happen

The wedding of Prince Salim — the same celebration that had once provided cover for Noor's assassination — arrived in her twenty-eighth year, eleven years into her rise, precisely matching the timeline of the coup she remembered from her first, unlived-again life.

She had spent a decade dismantling the coalition piece by piece, but she had learned, too well, not to assume any enemy was ever fully finished. On the night of the wedding, as fireworks lit the sky over the palace lake and the court's attention turned toward celebration, Noor did not retire early to her private chambers to accept a cup of wine from a trusted servant.

Instead, she stood at the edge of the celebration with Iskandar beside her — not sent away on a manufactured errand this time, because she had made certain, years in advance, that no order from the coalition's remaining sympathizers could pull him from her side without her immediate knowledge — and watched, with the particular cold satisfaction of a woman finally closing a decade-long account, as palace guards she had personally, quietly recruited over the preceding years intercepted a servant attempting to carry a cup of spiced wine toward her empty chambers.

The servant was not Fazil. She had made certain of that years ago. He was a minor page, bribed and frightened, who confessed within an hour to the remaining fragments of the Zahedi coalition's plot, provided with poison by a source that led, three interrogations later, directly back to the noble family she had spent a decade quietly, patiently defanging.

There was no coup that night. There was no poisoned cup that reached her lips. There was, instead, a quiet, efficient arrest carried out before the fireworks had even finished, while the wedding celebration continued, mercifully oblivious, on the lake beyond.
