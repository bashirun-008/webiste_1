Chapter Two: Who They'd Become

They had been, in college, an unlikely combination that made sense only in the specific alchemy of shared hostel corridors and 2 a.m. assignment deadlines. Priya, the group's relentless organizer even then, had wanted to run a company since she was nineteen and now, eight years later, genuinely did — a small but growing logistics startup that ate most of her waking hours and had, if she was honest with the group chat at 1 a.m. after two glasses of wine, eaten most of her actual life too.

Sameer had been the group's chaos agent — the one who'd once talked his way into three separate fake internships to avoid telling his parents he'd failed a semester, the one whose romantic life had been, throughout college, a genuinely alarming sequence of situationships he narrated with theatrical detail. Eight years on, he had job-hopped through six different companies, none of which he could fully explain leaving, and had developed a talent for making instability sound, in the retelling, like an adventure rather than what his mother, in increasingly worried phone calls, called "a crisis you're refusing to have properly."

Neha had married her college boyfriend, Rohit — not from the group, a mechanical engineering student she'd met at a fest in second year — two years after graduation, had a daughter now, and had quietly, without ever formally announcing it to the group, stopped mentioning the graphic design career she'd once talked about with the same intensity Priya talked about her startup. Nobody in the group had directly asked her about it in three years, a silence Neha had noticed and, in her lowest moments, resented them for.

Arjun had become, on paper, the most conventionally successful of the four — a senior product manager at a well-known tech company, a steady salary, a life that looked, from the outside, entirely sorted. What the group chat didn't know, because Arjun had never once told them, was that he'd spent the last year quietly unraveling under a workload and a manager who made him dread Monday mornings with a specificity that had started showing up as chest pains he hadn't yet seen a doctor about.

They arrived in Goa, all four of them, within six hours of each other on the same December Friday, and stood in the driveway of the rented villa Priya had booked, looking at each other for a long, strange moment before Sameer broke it by saying, "Okay, nobody's allowed to comment on how much older I look, we agreed on this rule telepathically just now," and the eight years of drift collapsed, instantly and completely, into the specific, undignified laughter of people who had once known each other better than anyone since.
