Chapter One: The Group That Never Dies

The WhatsApp group had been called, at various points over eight years, "Engineering Losers," "Not Engineers Anymore Thank God," "4 Idiots," and, since a chaotic New Year's Eve two years ago that none of them fully remembered, simply "CHAOS," all caps, non-negotiable.

It had four members: Priya Kulkarni, Sameer Joshi, Neha Rao, and Arjun Pillai, who had been inseparable for four years at an engineering college in Pune and had, in the eight years since graduating, drifted into the specific, low-grade estrangement that happens to most college friend groups — daily messages becoming weekly, then monthly, then reduced to birthday wishes and forwarded memes neither party actually read.

It was Priya who broke the silence, on a Tuesday in March, with a message that read simply: Kabir is getting married. Goa. December. We are ALL going. This is not a discussion.

Kabir had been the group's fifth member, technically, though less chaotic than the other four and therefore somehow always the one who ended up planning things. His engagement to a woman none of the group had met yet had apparently progressed, in the eighteen months since anyone last checked, all the way to a full destination wedding, and Priya — who had appointed herself, unelected, the group's permanent event coordinator — was not about to let eight years of drift end the streak of the four of them being in the same room together.

I have a toddler, Neha typed back within the hour. And a husband who thinks 'girls trip' means I'm going to a spa.

Tell him it's a work conference, Sameer suggested. I do this literally every time I want to play poker with strangers in Bangkok.

That's concerning on multiple levels, Arjun wrote, but also, yes, I'm in. I need to remember what my personality was like before I started saying things like "let's circle back" unironically at work.

By Thursday, all four had, with varying degrees of logistical chaos and marital negotiation, confirmed the trip.
