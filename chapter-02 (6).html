Chapter Eight: What Survives

Noor did not become empress in any formal sense — the emperor's health failed naturally, without incident, three years after the foiled coup, and the succession passed, as it should have, to Prince Salim, who ruled, with Noor's continued quiet counsel and Iskandar's newly restored and legitimate standing at court, a reign markedly more stable than the one she remembered from a life that no longer existed for anyone but her.

She married Iskandar in a modest ceremony that would have scandalized the court of her first life, where a woman of her rank marrying a man with no significant title would have been considered a waste of considerable political capital. In this second, hard-won life, she had already spent that capital protecting exactly the future she wanted rather than the one ambition alone would have dictated, and she found, standing beside him as the ceremony concluded, that she did not miss the throne she had once died defending.

"Do you ever think about what might have happened," Iskandar asked her once, years later, watching their daughter chase pigeons through the same palace gardens where he had once found her holding herself together after a fall from grace, "if you'd never come to that stable door, that first week?"

Noor thought of a poisoned cup of wine, a fireworks display over a lake, and a man sent away on a manufactured errand who never got the chance to save her because he was never given the chance to be trusted with the truth in time.

"I think about it," she said. "I think about it more than I let myself say. But I've decided the answer doesn't matter anymore, because it isn't the life we're living. This one is." She reached for his hand, the same steady, unwavering warmth that had once been the only trustworthy thing in an entire treacherous court, now simply the ordinary, extraordinary shape of her whole remaining life. "This is the crown I chose to keep. Everything else was just thorns."
