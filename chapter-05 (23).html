The Hill Behind the Mine

The tribal assembly seat of Salgaon, tucked into the fictional hill district of Amarkantha, had elected the same party for three terms running — not out of loyalty, but because nobody else had ever bothered to campaign there properly.

That changed the year Vasanth Oram, a schoolteacher's son from the Gond community, decided to contest, and the mining conglomerate Bhargava Minerals decided he needed to lose.

Bhargava Minerals had operated an open-cast bauxite mine on the edge of Salgaon for fifteen years under a lease the sitting MLA, Damodar Singh, had renewed twice without so much as a public hearing. In exchange, the company's local CSR wing had built two roads, a community hall, and — the year before the election — a shiny new sports stadium that nobody in Salgaon had asked for and few could afford to travel to on weekends.

Vasanth's campaign had no stadium to offer. It had a set of demands drawn up with village elders over eleven months of walking meetings: a public hearing on the mine's environmental clearance renewal, compensation arrears going back six years for displaced farming families, and a share of mining royalties routed directly to the village council instead of the district treasury, where it tended to vanish.

Damodar Singh, backed by Bhargava Minerals' considerable resources, ran a campaign built on the stadium, the roads, and a steady drumbeat of warnings that Vasanth's 'outside NGO friends' — a reference to two environmental lawyers who had helped draft his public hearing petition — wanted to shut the mine entirely and put four thousand daily-wage workers out of a job. It wasn't true, but it was repeated often enough, on enough local cable channels owned by a Bhargava subsidiary, that many believed it.

Three weeks before polling, Vasanth's motorcycle was found overturned on the ghat road with its tank empty, though he swore he'd filled it that morning. He wasn't hurt, having walked the last four kilometers, but he understood the message. He didn't report it to the police, whose superintendent had attended Damodar Singh's daughter's wedding the previous month. He mentioned it once, briefly, at his next meeting, and let the crowd draw its own conclusion.

What Bhargava Minerals hadn't accounted for was the women's self-help group network that ran through every village in the constituency, originally organized around microloans for handloom weaving. Vasanth's wife, Suman, was a member of one; through her, the campaign reached households no male canvasser could enter comfortably, carrying not slogans but the arrears ledger itself — the actual, itemized, six-year-old compensation numbers, village by village, that the district administration had never bothered to make public.

On the ground, the contest came down to a simple, brutal arithmetic: the stadium reached perhaps three thousand voters who could afford leisure and travel. The arrears ledger reached eleven thousand households who were owed money and had never seen it. Vasanth won by two thousand one hundred and forty votes, a margin the district collector's office initially disputed before a recount confirmed it.

Bhargava Minerals' regional director flew in within a week, not to concede but to negotiate — offering Vasanth, still unsworn, a seat on a new 'community advisory board' with a modest honorarium attached. Vasanth said he'd be glad to sit on it, as an elected representative, provided the public hearing happened first and the arrears were paid before the board's first meeting. The director left without agreeing to either, but also without the confidence he'd arrived with.

The public hearing was eventually held, seven months into Vasanth's term, after he tabled the environmental clearance renewal as an assembly question three sessions running. It did not go the mine's way. Bhargava Minerals kept its lease, but only after agreeing to a royalty-sharing formula that sent a fixed percentage directly to the Salgaon village council — a formula Vasanth had drafted personally, on the back of the same walking-meeting notebook he'd carried for eleven months before anyone thought he had a chance.

The arrears took longer. Bhargava Minerals' lawyers contested the six-year figure line by line, arguing that several of the displaced families had already received 'in-kind compensation' in the form of the community hall and sports stadium built years earlier — an argument the district court eventually rejected, ruling that infrastructure built for the company's own CSR obligations could not retroactively substitute for cash owed to specific displaced households. It took two more years, and three separate hearings, before the first installment reached the village council's account.

By then, Vasanth had learned that Bhargava Minerals did not fight only in courtrooms. In his second year, the company quietly funded a rival candidate for the block-level panchayat elections, a young man from Vasanth's own community named Bikash Munda, whose campaign — for a seat that controlled almost nothing but symbolized a great deal — was better funded than Vasanth's own assembly run had been. Bikash lost narrowly, but the message was received: the company intended to build its own local leadership inside Salgaon rather than continue negotiating with Vasanth's.

Suman, whose self-help group network had carried the original arrears ledger door to door, became the quiet answer to that strategy. She expanded the network's mandate beyond microloans into a structured system of monthly village meetings where royalty disbursements, employment rosters at the mine, and environmental compliance reports were read aloud and explained line by line — turning what had been a campaign tool into a permanent institution of local oversight that no single defeated candidate, however well-funded, could easily dismantle.

Damodar Singh, the MLA Vasanth had defeated, did not vanish from Salgaon politics either. He resurfaced eighteen months later as a 'community liaison officer' on Bhargava Minerals' own payroll, a role that paid considerably better than his old assembly salary and required him only to do what he had already spent two decades doing: smoothing the company's relationship with whichever local structures of power happened to matter that year. Vasanth found this less infuriating than instructive — proof that the mine's real strategy had never been about any one candidate, but about ensuring someone, anyone, remained willing to manage Salgaon on its behalf.

By the end of his first term, Vasanth had not shut down the mine, had not become wealthy from it, and had not fully escaped the quiet, permanent surveillance that came with being the man who'd once found his motorcycle's tank mysteriously empty. What he had built, instead, was a village council that received its royalty share on schedule, a self-help group network that read the company's own compliance reports back to it in open meetings, and a habit, among eleven thousand households, of expecting to see the ledger before believing the promise. It was not the end of the fight. It was, he told Suman on the night the first installment finally arrived, simply the first year they'd fought it on numbers instead of on faith.
