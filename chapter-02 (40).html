Chapter 3: The Projector Room

To escape his son's clinical logic, Imtiaz climbed the narrow, winding concrete steps that led to the highest point of the theatre—the projection box. The staircase was dark, illuminated only by thin slits of sunlight cutting through the brickwork. As he pushed open the heavy asbestos door, the temperature spiked, but the air grew immediately familiar. This was the heart of the machine.

Inside, surrounded by heavy iron reels, canisters of vintage film cleaner, and old copper wiring, sat Santosh. The seventy-year-old projectionist was leaning over a heavy, olive-green Soviet-era stereophonic projector, his thumb gently smoothing a strip of old 35mm celluloid film.

"He wants to sell, doesn't he?" Santosh asked without turning around, his ears attuned to the specific click of Imtiaz's leather sandals.

"He says we are preserving a corpse, Santosh," Imtiaz said, sitting on an overturned wooden crate. He picked up a small, empty aluminum film canister, turning it over in his hands.

Santosh laughed, a dry, raspy sound from a lifetime of inhaling carbon smoke. "Let the young boys talk about corpses, Imtiaz. They don't know what it's like to hold light in your hands. Remember 1975? When the Amitabh Bachchan film opened on Diwali? The line for the advance booking went all the way down to the Urdu Bazaar crossing. The police had to use sticks just to keep the crowd from breaking the glass doors."

They sat in silence for a long time, listening to the distant, muted roar of the old Delhi traffic outside. The old projector was a beautiful monster of steel and gears, an artifact from a time when cinema was an event, a communal ritual, a shared dream. Now, the digital projector next to it—a small, sterile black box installed to play modern digital satellite feeds—looked like an appliance, cold and devoid of soul.

"The commercial electricity bill came this morning," Imtiaz said quietly, breaking the reverie. "It is more than our total ticket sales for the last three months. We cannot afford the weekend screenings anymore, old friend."

Santosh's hands stopped moving over the film reel. He looked out through the tiny projection port down at the dark, vast expanse of the empty auditorium below. "Then let us give them one last show, Imtiaz-bhai. A real show. With the old arc lamps."
