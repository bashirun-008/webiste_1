Chapter Six: What Loyalty Costs

Iskandar's fall — the disgrace she remembered from her first life, engineered three years into her rise — came anyway, in a smaller, less devastating form, because some currents in a court this treacherous could be redirected but never entirely stopped. The coalition, sensing an ally worth removing, maneuvered to have him stripped of his stable administration post and reassigned to a remote garrison, a demotion dressed up as a routine posting.

This time, Noor did not lose track of him the way her past-self, still learning the shape of her own power, once had. She used the political capital she had spent years building to have the reassignment quietly blocked, framing it to the emperor as a matter of stable efficiency rather than personal favor, careful never to let her intervention look like what it actually was: a woman protecting a man she could not yet admit, even to herself, that she loved.

"You saved my position," Iskandar said, finding her in the palace gardens the evening the reassignment was reversed. "I know it was you. The efficiency argument was well constructed, but I've watched you work long enough now to recognize your handwriting on a maneuver, even when your name isn't on it."

"I protect useful people," Noor said, deflecting, though her voice betrayed more than she intended.

"Is that what I am to you? Useful?"

She turned to face him properly, and found she could not, this time, hide behind the calculated distance that had once been her only available survival strategy. "You are the only person in this entire court whose loyalty I have never once had to question or manage or purchase," she said. "In a place built entirely out of transactions, you have never once asked me for anything. I don't know what to call that except the single most valuable thing I have ever been given, and I have spent years now terrified of naming it as anything more than usefulness, because the moment I call it what it actually is, I become vulnerable to losing it."

"You won't lose it," Iskandar said quietly. "I've loved you since the third week I knew you, Noor, when you stood at my stable door and told me things about grain ledgers you had no reasonable way of knowing, and I decided I didn't need the explanation to know that whatever you were, I trusted you completely. That hasn't wavered once in six years. It's not going to start wavering now because you've finally said it aloud."
