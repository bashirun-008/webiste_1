Chapter Two: The Coach Who Didn't Want the Job

Ranbir Ahluwalia had once, briefly, played first-class cricket for Punjab — a promising fast bowler whose career had ended at twenty-six, not through injury but through a selection scandal he'd never fully recovered from, watching a place on the state team go to a less talented but better-connected player while Ranbir's own case disappeared into the specific, unaccountable machinery of a cricket board more interested in politics than merit.

He had spent the fifteen years since in a slow, bitter retreat from the sport that had once been his entire identity, working now as a low-level coaching assistant at a private academy across the city that paid him to run drills for children whose parents could afford fees Ranbir's own working-class family could never have managed, a job he did with competent, joyless detachment.

He first encountered the Sector 12 kids by accident, cutting through their dusty ground on a shortcut home from the academy, and stopped, despite himself, to watch Deepak bowl an unpolished but genuinely dangerous yorker that would have troubled a batsman twice Deepak's experience.

"Who taught you that action?" Ranbir asked, unable to help himself.

"Nobody," Deepak said, faintly defensive, as though expecting to be mocked. "I just watch videos on Vicky's brother's phone and copy what looks right."

Ranbir stayed for twenty more minutes that day, watching the whole ragtag group with a growing, uncomfortable recognition of raw, unrefined talent being wasted for lack of exactly the kind of structured coaching he'd spent fifteen years bitterly providing to children who, by contrast, mostly didn't need it nearly as badly.

He did not offer to coach them that day, or the next. It took Vicky — persistent in the specific, shameless way twelve-year-olds can be — showing up at the private academy three separate times over two weeks, asking, with total straight-faced confidence, whether "the fast bowling uncle" would consider coaching a real team, before Ranbir, more out of exhaustion than conviction, agreed to a single trial session.

"One session," he told Vicky. "Don't expect anything beyond that."
