Chapter Seven: What Gets Rebuilt

They exchanged the letters, eventually — not that night, both too raw for it, but two weeks later, by post, the way the original correspondence had always been conducted, a small, deliberate act of returning to the specific medium that had once carried their friendship across a genuine distance.

Reading Rahul's letter, thirteen years late, Farhan understood, for the first time, exactly how alone his best friend had felt during the year Farhan had assumed Rahul was simply thriving in his glamorous new city, too busy for the friendship that had once defined him. Reading Farhan's letter, Rahul understood, with a grief that arrived thirteen years too late to do anything but ache, exactly how badly his best friend had needed him during the worst year of his life, and how completely Rahul's own fear of seeming pathetic had cost them both a chance to actually be there for each other when it mattered most.

They did not, in the months that followed, magically restore the daily, effortless closeness of two eight-year-olds sharing a last bench — that particular, uncomplicated intimacy belonged, they both came to accept, to a specific, unrepeatable season of life. What they built instead was something more deliberate: monthly phone calls that neither let slide the way the letters once had, an annual trip back to Nagpur that Rahul, now settled with his own family in Mumbai, made a permanent fixture of his calendar, a friendship rebuilt not on the accident of shared geography that had made it easy the first time, but on the conscious, repeated choice both men now understood a real friendship, at their age, actually required.

On Rahul's fortieth birthday, ten years after the reunion, Farhan flew to Mumbai — his own life having taken him, eventually, to Nagpur's growing IT sector, married with two kids of his own — and the two men, sitting on Rahul's apartment balcony with the Mumbai skyline spread out below them, found themselves, unprompted, resuming the old fifteen-year argument about Sachin Tendulkar versus Rahul Dravid with exactly the same total, joyful investment they'd brought to it at fourteen.

"You know," Farhan said, "we're never actually going to resolve this argument."

"I know," Rahul said. "I think that might be the whole point. Some things you're not supposed to finish. You're just supposed to keep having them, for as long as you get to."

Somewhere in Nagpur, the classroom that had once held the last bench of Row C had long since been renovated beyond recognition, the specific wooden bench itself almost certainly discarded decades ago in some routine school clearance neither man would ever know the date of. But the particular, unrepeatable friendship it had once housed — interrupted, nearly lost, deliberately, imperfectly rebuilt — had outlasted the furniture entirely, the way the truest things from childhood, if you're lucky enough and stubborn enough to fight for them, sometimes do.
