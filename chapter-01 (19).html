Chapter 4: The Sound of the Loom

Two days before the final results of the preliminary video audit were due, the rain returned with a vengeance, knocking out the town's electricity grid. The hills fell into an atmospheric darkness, illuminated only by candles and the orange glow of kerosene lamps. To make matters worse, Azungla fell severely ill, her chest heavy with a persistent winter cough that left her bedridden and fragile.

Arenla stayed by her side, tending to the fire and preparing warm ginger infusions. The house was dead quiet, save for the constant, steady drumbeat of heavy monsoon rain against the zinc sheets above. Without the distraction of her phone—which had long since run out of battery—Arenla found her senses expanding to fill the domestic silence.

She sat in the main room next to her grandmother's abandoned handloom. In the dim candlelight, the half-finished Ao Naga shawl looked like a living tapestry. She reached out and touched the rough, indigo-dyed wool. She noticed the slight imperfections, the minor variations in tension, the human errors that gave the fabric its unique character.

As she sat there, her mind began to replay the choreography of her audition dance. Strangely, the syncopated, modern beats of the Korean pop song began to align perfectly with the imaginary rhythm of her grandmother's weaving. Clack, slide, press. Step, pivot, snap. It wasn't a contradiction; it was the exact same pursuit of structure, geometry, and expression. The traditional loom wasn't an antithesis to her modern ambitions—it was simply an older, more enduring technology of storytelling.

Azungla stirred from her bed, her eyes opening slightly to look at her granddaughter sitting by the machine. "You have your mother's shoulders, Arenla," she whispered, her voice weak but clear. "She could never sit still either. She always wanted to see what was behind the next hill. Go wild, child. But remember that the mountains don't move just because you leave them."
