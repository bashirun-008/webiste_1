Chapter 3: What the Ash Remembers

The archive room smelled like every archive room Mira had ever worked in — dust, cold metal, and the particular staleness of air that had been recycled through the same filters for two decades.

She found the KH-9's original mission logs on the third shelf, in a box that hadn't been opened since before she'd joined the program. The satellite had flown one final assignment before decommissioning: three weeks mapping a wildfire's spread across a mountain range that no longer had a name on current maps, because the fire had taken the town that named it.

Ashes of tomorrow, someone had written on the mission folder, in handwriting she didn't recognize. A joke, maybe. Or a warning nobody had taken seriously.

She scanned the final images the satellite had ever taken — the last pictures before its cameras went dark and its purpose, officially, ended. Smoke shaped like a slow wound across green. A single road, cutting through the burn, unused.

And there, in the metadata nobody had ever bothered to read closely: a compression signature that matched, almost exactly, the shape of the transmission she'd found three nights ago.

The satellite wasn't inventing a new language. It was remembering an old one — something embedded in its final assignment, dormant for eleven years, waking up now for a reason she didn't yet understand.

Mira sat back on her heels in the dust and the cold light, and felt the particular vertigo of realizing a question was bigger than she'd thought.

What were you really watching up there, she thought, and who told you to keep watching after we stopped listening?

Somewhere above her, in a graveyard orbit nobody had mapped twice, the signal pulsed again. Four minutes. Twelve seconds. Waiting.
