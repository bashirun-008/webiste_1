Chapter Three: The First Night, and the Cracks

The first night was, as these things always are, mostly nostalgia — old stories retold with the specific exaggeration eight years grants a memory, old nicknames resurrected, a group photo taken in the exact stupid pose they'd used for every photo in college that made all four of them look, according to Sameer, "criminally unhirable."

The cracks showed up on the second night, over too much sundowner rum, the way real conversations often arrive disguised as jokes until suddenly they aren't.

It started when Sameer mentioned, breezily, that he was "between things" again, having left his latest job two months ago over what he described as "creative differences with a company that refused to understand my vision," and Priya, three drinks in and tired in a way that had nothing to do with the trip, said, more sharply than she meant to, "Sameer, you've had six jobs in eight years. At what point does 'creative differences' just become 'I get bored and run'?"

The silence that followed was the kind that used to happen instantly and dissolve just as fast in college, back when nobody had learned yet to hold a grudge past breakfast. Now it sat there, heavier, because they were no longer nineteen-year-olds who saw each other every day and could repair things in real time.

"That's a little harsh," Neha said quietly, though whether she was defending Sameer or simply uncomfortable with confrontation, even she wasn't sure.

"It's not harsh, it's true," Sameer said, and the theatrical lightness he usually wrapped around himself slipped, just slightly. "You think I don't know that? You think I like explaining to my parents every eighteen months why the thing I was so sure about didn't work out? At least I'm trying things, Priya. Some of us didn't get to just build one company and have it work."

"That's not fair," Priya said, stung. "You think it 'just worked'? You have no idea what the last four years have actually cost me."

"Then tell us," Arjun said, quiet, and everyone turned to him, slightly startled, because Arjun rarely inserted himself into anything. "Tell us what it's cost you. Actually tell us. Because I've noticed none of us have told each other anything real in about six years, and I'm starting to think that's not an accident."
