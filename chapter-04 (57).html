Chapter Seven: What Endures

The Underdogs did not, in the years that followed, become a legendary team that dominated Chandigarh's junior cricket circuit — Ranbir's coaching, however dedicated, could not fully compensate for years of resource gaps the wealthier academies had built into their programs from the start.

What the team did produce, over the following six years, was a specific, quiet transformation in the trajectory of each of the eight kids' individual lives that had far less to do with cricket trophies than with the confidence, discipline, and belief in themselves that four months of being taken seriously by an adult who refused to see their circumstances as a limitation had planted in each of them.

Sunny, whose mother's fears about cricket distracting from academics had proven entirely unfounded, eventually earned a spot at a respected engineering college on a merit scholarship, crediting, in an interview for a local newspaper years later covering the Underdogs' modest but real legacy, the specific discipline and pressure-management skills Ranbir's coaching had built in him as directly responsible for his ability to handle competitive academic pressure without crumbling.

Deepak did not become a professional cricketer, in the end, but the rebuilt bowling technique Ranbir had painstakingly constructed earned him a place on his college's cricket team, and the specific confidence of having once beaten genuine odds carried, his own mother observed, into every other area of a life that could easily, without that summer, have settled for considerably less ambition.

Anjali, whose father had once considered cricket "unsuitable," became, eventually, a state-level junior player, her father, by then, her most vocal supporter, having long since forgotten his own initial resistance in the face of watching his daughter's specific, hard-won excellence.

Vicky, the loud, undersized boy who had declared them the Underdogs so everyone would already expect a surprise, eventually became, at nineteen, an assistant coach himself at a community program in a different underserved sector of the city — a decision he attributed, without hesitation, to a specific, formative summer when a bitter, unemployed former first-class bowler had chosen to see eight overlooked kids from Sector 12 as worth every unpaid hour of his time.

Ranbir himself, whatever bitterness fifteen years of an unjust career had built in him, found, in the years of continued coaching that followed the tournament — the Underdogs becoming, eventually, an established, if modest, community cricket program rather than a single summer's experiment — something he'd assumed the cricket board's old betrayal had permanently taken from him: the specific, uncomplicated joy of the game itself, rediscovered not through his own unfulfilled ambitions, but through eight kids from a dusty ground behind a water tank who had taught him, without ever meaning to, that the sport had never actually been the problem. It had only ever been the specific, small-minded gatekeeping of people who decided, arbitrarily, who deserved a real chance to play it.
