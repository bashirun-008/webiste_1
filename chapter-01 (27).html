Chapter Two: The Shape of a Whole Childhood

Their friendship was built, as the best childhood friendships are, out of an accumulation of small, specific things rather than any single grand event. Farhan taught Rahul to ride a bicycle, running alongside him down their lane for three straight evenings until Rahul finally stopped wobbling, and Rahul taught Farhan, patiently, over an entire monsoon season, how to actually understand long division instead of just copying Rahul's homework, a debt Farhan repaid for the rest of school by defending Rahul from the occasional bully with a loyalty that was, itself, a kind of love neither boy would have used that word for.

They had a shared cricket obsession that neither set of parents fully understood the depth of — a complete, meticulously maintained collection of player cards, a ritual of watching every India match together on Rahul's family's television because Farhan's family didn't have cable, a running fifteen-year argument about whether Sachin Tendulkar or Rahul Dravid was the superior batsman that neither boy ever actually wanted resolved because the arguing itself was the point.

They knew each other's families the way only childhood friends do — Rahul ate at Farhan's house often enough that Farhan's mother kept a specific dietary note pinned to her fridge reminding herself Rahul didn't like too much chili; Farhan spent so many nights at Rahul's house during exam seasons that Rahul's father, a reserved man not given to displays of affection, had started, without ever formally announcing it, treating Farhan like a second son, correcting his cricket stance in the lane outside their house with the same patient attention he gave his own boy.

They fought, occasionally, the way close friends do — a spectacular, weeklong silence in Class 8 over a girl neither of them, in retrospect, had actually liked that much, a smaller falling-out in Class 10 when Farhan borrowed and lost Rahul's cherished autographed cricket bat — but the fights never lasted, because the friendship underneath them was sturdier than any single grievance, a fact both boys understood instinctively even before they had the language to articulate it.
