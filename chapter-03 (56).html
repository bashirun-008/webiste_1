Chapter Six: The Final

The Underdogs of Sector 12 reached the tournament's final against a considerably better-funded team from a wealthier sector — boys who had grown up with proper coaching academies, matching kits, and the specific, unbothered confidence of children who had never once had to fight for access to the sport they were playing.

It was, on paper, a mismatch. It felt, to the eight kids from Sector 12, standing on the boundary of a proper turf ground far nicer than their own dusty patch behind the water tank, exactly like every mismatch they'd quietly absorbed as the ordinary condition of their entire lives, from school resources to family finances to the casual, unexamined confidence wealthier kids carried into every room.

They lost the final. Not badly — a genuinely close match that came down to the final over, Deepak's rebuilt yorker producing one more crucial wicket before the opposing team's last batsman, calmer under pressure than any twelve-year-old had any right to be, hit the two runs needed to win with a single ball to spare.

The eight of them stood on the boundary afterward, gutted, several of them crying openly with the specific, uncomplicated devastation only children allow themselves, and Ranbir, watching them, found himself confronting something he'd spent fifteen bitter years avoiding: the choice between letting this loss become, for these kids, one more confirmation of the world's unfairness, or making it mean something else entirely.

"Listen to me," he said, gathering all eight of them at the boundary, his own voice rougher than he intended. "You did not lose today because you weren't good enough. You lost by two runs to a team that has had proper coaching since they were six years old, with equipment your families could never have afforded, on grounds like this one that your dusty patch behind the water tank could never compete with in resources. You closed a gap in four months that should have taken years to close. I know that, because I spent fifteen years watching kids exactly like the ones you just played, and none of them, not one, would have closed that gap as fast as you just did." He looked at each of them in turn. "This is not the story of a team that lost. This is the story of a team that proved, in four months, exactly how much talent this sector has always had, that nobody outside it ever bothered to notice until now. That doesn't end today. That's just getting started."
