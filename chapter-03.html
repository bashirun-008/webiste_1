Chapter 1: Arrival

The satellite had been dead for eleven years before it started talking again.

Mira found the transmission the way she found most things worth finding — by accident, at three in the morning, half-asleep over a console that was never meant to hold her attention this long. A single line of telemetry, blinking where no telemetry should be. KH-9, decommissioned 2041, drifting in a graveyard orbit nobody had bothered to map twice.

She sat up. The coffee beside her had gone cold an hour ago, and the station around her had gone quiet the way it always did past midnight — vents humming, nothing else. She pulled the signal apart line by line, expecting noise, expecting the kind of cosmic static that made rookies see ghosts in every dataset.

It wasn't noise.

It repeated. Every four minutes and twelve seconds, precise as a heartbeat, a burst of structured data folded into a shape she almost recognized. Almost. She'd spent six years reading dead machines and this one was doing something none of them did anymore.

It was learning.

"You're not supposed to be here," she said, to no one, to the empty room, to a piece of aluminum and silicon that had been falling through the dark since before she'd finished her doctorate.

The console blinked once, as if it had heard her.

By morning she had a decision to make, and she already knew she'd made it hours ago — somewhere around the second cup of coffee, somewhere around the moment the signal changed shape to answer a question she hadn't asked yet.

She was going to find out what was still awake up there. Whatever it cost.
